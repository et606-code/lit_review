Educational Technology, IIT Bombay

The Assignment Task

This assignment focused on understanding how different digital tools support researchers across the entire literature review workflow. The emphasis was on comparing GenAI-based tools (such as Scispace, Consensus, Novix and Elicit) with more traditional or visual tools (such as Google Scholar, Research Rabbit, Connected Papers and H5P).

Each tool was explored hands-on with real research queries. For every tool, the evaluation considered usability, quality of results, transparency of AI output, ability to support critical reading, and how well the tool fit into specific stages of the literature review.

Core Research Question: ‚ÄúHow do emerging GenAI tools for literature review compare with traditional and visual tools in terms of their strengths, limitations, and best-fit stages within the literature review process?‚Äù

The website below summarises this comparative analysis, shows how tools align with different stages of the workflow, and provides an interactive way to explore which tool is most suitable for a particular purpose.

5 Key Stages of the Literature Review Workflow

The literature review is not a single activity. It is a cycle of searching, organising, understanding, connecting and communicating. The tools below were mapped to these five stages so that GenAI tools can be compared not only with each other, but also with traditional databases and visual mapping tools.

1

Searching & Discovery

Clarifying initial keywords, identifying seminal authors and locating both classic and recent papers. GenAI tools are used here mainly for quick overviews and checking relevance of search results.

Main tools: Google Scholar, Consensus

2

Organizing & Managing

Screening titles and abstracts, clustering papers by theme, and tracking what has already been read. AI-based report views in tools like Novix and Scispace support structured note-making and concept grouping.

Main tools: Novix, Scispace (workspace features)

3

Visualizing Connections

Making sense of how papers are connected: who cites whom, which themes are emerging, and where there are gaps. Visual tools give an overview that pure text-based GenAI summaries cannot provide.

Main tools: Research Rabbit, Connected Papers

4

Analyzing & Synthesizing

Reading in depth, extracting comparable information (context, method, sample, findings) and synthesising across studies. At this stage, GenAI tools act as assistants: they speed up extraction but still require human judgement.

Main tools: Scispace, Novix, Elicit

5

Writing & Presentation

Transforming insights into a coherent narrative and sharing findings in an engaging way. AI tools help in structuring sections, while interactive tools such as H5P support quizzes, presentations and knowledge checks.

Main tools: Scispace (writing support), H5P

Google Scholar

üìö Traditional

Best for: Initial discovery

Start here when you are not sure which keywords or authors are central. Combine keyword search with ‚Äúcited by‚Äù and time filters to move from broad to focused.

Usage tips
  • Use quotation marks for exact phrases.
  • Sort by ‚Äúsince year‚Äù to catch recent work.
  • Export shortlisted items to your reference manager.

Scispace

ü§ñ GenAI

Best for: Deep reading and drafting

Upload PDFs to receive structured explanations of methods, results and key terms. Use the ‚Äúask‚Äù feature to clarify unfamiliar concepts before writing.

Usage tips
  • Always verify AI-generated answers with the original section.
  • Highlight and annotate passages you plan to cite directly.
  • Use generated outlines only as a starting point for your own structure.

Novix

ü§ñ GenAI

Best for: Structuring the review

Guides you through stages like collecting papers, clustering themes and generating draft paragraphs. Helpful when you already have a set of PDFs and want to move towards a first full draft.

Usage tips
  • Manually correct AI-generated labels for clusters.
  • Export reports and rephrase them in your own academic voice.
  • Use it to identify missing strands that require more searching.

Consensus

ü§ñ GenAI

Best for: Quick evidence check

Ask natural-language questions and see short answers supported by cited studies. Useful to check whether a pattern you notice is already well-established or still uncertain.

Usage tips
  • Use specific, focused questions instead of very broad ones.
  • Open the underlying papers for any claim that looks surprising.
  • Avoid copying the AI summary directly into your writing.

Research Rabbit

üï∏Ô∏è Visual

Best for: Exploring networks

Drop in a key paper and explore clusters of related work. Helps to identify author networks, related topics and potential gaps where fewer nodes appear.

Usage tips
  • Begin with a highly cited, clearly relevant seed paper.
  • Use collections to separate different sub-themes.
  • Export lists of nodes to your reference manager for later reading.

Connected Papers

üï∏Ô∏è Visual

Best for: Quick structural map

Generates a single graph centred on your seed paper, showing prior and derivative works. Ideal for a snapshot of where a particular study sits in the wider conversation.

Usage tips
  • Try multiple seed papers if the first graph looks too sparse.
  • Use filters to focus on a specific time window.
  • Note which regions of the graph you have not yet explored.

Elicit

ü§ñ GenAI

Best for: Comparison tables

Builds tables from multiple papers, letting you compare participants, interventions, measures and findings side-by-side. This is very helpful when preparing a synthesis matrix.

Usage tips
  • Customise the table columns to match your research questions.
  • Double-check extracted values such as sample size and instruments.
  • Export tables and refine them manually before using in a report.

H5P

üï∏Ô∏è Interactive

Best for: Sharing results

Use H5P to transform key insights from your review into interactive slides, quizzes or timelines. This is especially useful for teaching or for communicating findings to non-expert audiences.

Usage tips
  • Design short knowledge checks based on your main themes.
  • Use images of citation maps or tables to support explanations.
  • Keep interaction simple so that focus stays on the underlying research.

Key Insights from the Comparative Analysis

  • GenAI tools are most powerful in the mid-stages of the workflow (analysis, organisation and early drafting). They accelerate reading and pattern spotting, but still depend on human critical judgement.
  • Traditional databases remain essential for reliable, transparent discovery of peer-reviewed work. GenAI search is a supplement, not a replacement.
  • Visual tools fill a gap that GenAI cannot fully cover: they show structure at a glance and help identify clusters, bridges and gaps in the literature.
  • Best results come from a hybrid pipeline where each tool is used for what it is genuinely good at, instead of forcing a single GenAI platform to do everything.
  • Responsible use of GenAI is critical: every AI-generated summary or claim must be cross-checked with the original paper, and citations should always be verified manually.
